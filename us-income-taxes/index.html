Retirement, credits & capital gains

A 401(k) is an employer-sponsored retirement account that lets you invest pre- or post-tax dollars with tax advantages. Money grows tax-free while it's in the account.

For 2025, the IRS elective deferral limit used on the slider below is $23,500.

The core tradeoff between the two types

Traditional (pre-tax): You contribute before taxes, lowering your taxable income now. You pay taxes when you withdraw in retirement. Better if you expect to be in a lower tax bracket later.

Roth (post-tax): You contribute after taxes, so no upfront benefit. But withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free. Better if you expect to be in a higher bracket later — or just want tax-free income in retirement.

About this model

Educational visualization. Federal tax uses IRS 2025 ordinary income brackets; Child Tax Credit includes non-refundable credit and income phase-out only (not the refundable additional child tax credit). Saver’s Credit is simplified. State tax: Utah (4.5% flat + taxpayer credit phase-out), California (2025 brackets + 1% mental health surtax + exemption credits), Idaho (5.3% flat with small zero bracket), New York (2025 state brackets), Oregon (2025 brackets + federal tax subtraction + exemption credit), Arizona (2.5% flat + 25% LTCG subtraction), Nevada/Texas/Washington (no state income tax). Long-term capital gains use 2025 preferential federal rates (0% / 15% / 20%) stacked on ordinary taxable income. Net capital losses offset up to $3,000 of ordinary income per year (carryforward shown, not modeled further). The allocation chart reflects wages only. Includes employee Social Security and Medicare only—not employer payroll tax.
